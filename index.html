The play uses marriage and its aspects, mainly how society expects and enforces marriage as a tradition, and ultimately an end goal for all. The main message in A Doll's House may be that a true marriage involves the joining of equals. The play centres on the dissolution of a marriage that doesn't meet the standards expected by society at the time.

Gender is often said to be but a performance (originally said by feminist philosopher Judith Butler in their 1990 book Gender Trouble). This is demonstrated prominently in The play, especially in regards to how Nora and Torvald behave in their marriage. Throughout the play, Nora and Torvald are performing acts even when no one is watching, the audience themselves. This behavior was taught to Nora from a young age by her father, and hence is all that she ever knows about her life.

Torvald's role in the story is the typical male behavior in a marriage, meaning he is the breadwinner as well as a protector of Nora. He earns money for the household, and in turn treats Nora as a "reward". He gets to play with Nora, and she is nothing but an interactive doll within his eyes, playing the role of the perfect woman for him. In this sense, Nora is just a representation of womanhood that Torvald enjoys.

Money plays a big role in this play, as it symbolises the power the characters have over one another. It is money that drives the health and wealth of society, but it has the capability to tear apart tightly knit relationships, particularly between Helmer and Nora. Without money, one cannot sustain themselves, as seen in Mrs Linde's case. Henrik Ibsen uses money as the root of many of the problems encountered by characters in this play.

One example is how Torvald's position affects how everyone around him perceives and acts around him. In the beginning of the play, Mrs. Linde seeks out Nora in order to get a job. Most importantly, Dr. Rank now perceives himself as a burden and thinks of himself as worthless to Torvald, now thinking that Torvald would be disgusted by a mere sighting of him.

Just like the ornament, Nora's and Torvald's marraige is an empty shell. It does not contain any weight on the inside, and rather functions to look "pretty" to satisfy the gazing eyes of society.

Macaroons are used to symbolise Nora's attempt to regain control of her life choices over Torvald. She eats them whenever Torvald is not around, and eating macaroons is how Nora is able to be herself in spite of her situation. The macaroons also symbolises Nora's disobedience. One example is in the beginning pages, where she eats a macaroon or two, even though she knows that Torvald doesn't allow her to do so and that she will face consequences.

The first letter mentioned in the book symbolises the truth about Nora's past and initiates the inevitable dissolution of her marriage. The second releases Nora from her obligation to lie on behalf of Krogstad, as well as her obligation to Torvald. The mailbox serves to foreshadow life-changing events throughout the story, and its locking shows that Torvald is controlling as only he has the “key”.

The Tarantella that Nora and Torvald hold in Act III is the last thing Nora does for Torvald before her secret is revealed. Here, she does two things - request for Torvald to make her a dress and prepare one of her own. She asks to be dolled; to be controlled, whilst also displaying a sense of agency, this time she displays it outwardly, allowing all to see. A fitting resolution for what she think is the end to her marriage.
